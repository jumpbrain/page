Data Scientist at War: Part 2

Another useful thought model
August 26, 2025 · 6 min

Introduction#

You’re deployed as an analyst in a country called Extremiststan. The FOB at which you sit is located a few miles south of the main city, Dystopolis. Tonight, some of your high-speed colleagues are going to meet with a prospective source. They want your judgement on which route to take.

There are three roads leading into Dystopolis, the horizontal road, the vertical road and the diagonal road.

To answer this you scrape incident reports via some pre-defined social media channels that you’re currently tracking as well as police reports sourced from the local department. You aggregate the last year of data for each road.

The vertical road had a spike in incidents over the last two weeks. In the last 72 hours, exactly zero reported incidents on that road. Perfect, you run a quick calculation, the vertical road has the lowest probability of incidents in the next 12 hours. You recommend it.

Only after your recommendation do you notice two details:

The vertical road has been red listed by local police. They no longer respond there. A local news agency publicly announced they stopped sending crews there 72 hours ago. Your collegaues have already left.

The next morning they return, in three body bags.

When events are rare, messy, or selectively observed, think in terms of a latent risk surface, not raw counts.

This example shows up in conflict zones, cyber incidents, fraud, medical risk, and reliability engineering.

First things first, when we think of count data, e.g number of incidents on a road during some time span, it’s often helpful to think of the counts as manifestations of some underlying risk surface that we cannot directly observe (that’s what latent means).

Method#

We build a minimal Bayesian model that separates:

  1. How much exposure each road segment has.
  2. How risky that road or day is, after adjusting for exposure.
  3. How information should be pooled across roads and days when data are sparse.

Data#

In our Extremiststan toy data:

  • Each row is a road segment on a given day.
  • We observe:
    • incidents: number of incidents on that segment-day.
    • road: which of the three roads.
    • segment: segment ID along the road.
    • day: index for the day.
    • x, y: coordinates for plotting.
    • exposure: a proxy for how much “opportunity” there was for something bad to happen.

You can think of exposure as:

  • number of vehicles using that segment,
  • patrol presence,
  • or simply “how much of that road was observable and reportable.”

If exposure collapses (no one drives there, no one reports from there), then even a very dangerous road will appear quiet in the raw counts.

Model#

We model incident counts as Poisson outcomes driven by a latent rate:

[ y_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu_i) ]

with

[ \log \mu_i = \alpha

  • u_{\text{road}[i]}
  • v_{\text{day}[i]}
  • \beta_{\text{exp}} \cdot \text{zlogexp}_i ]

where:

  • (\alpha): global baseline log-rate.
  • (u_{\text{road}}): road-specific deviations (hierarchical, sum-to-zero).
  • (v_{\text{day}}): day-specific deviations (hierarchical, sum-to-zero).
  • (\text{zlogexp}_i): standardized log-exposure for that segment-day.
  • (\beta_{\text{exp}}): effect of exposure on incident counts.

Priors (kept weakly informative and interpretable):

  • (\alpha \sim \mathcal{N}(-4, 1)): starts us in a plausible low baseline range for rare incidents.
  • (\sigma_{\text{road}} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(0.5)): typical road-to-road differences are modest unless data demand otherwise.
  • (\sigma_{\text{day}} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(0.2)): daily noise is present but not allowed to explode without evidence.
  • (u_{\text{road}}, v_{\text{day}}) are centered to sum to zero: they represent deviations, not duplicated intercepts.
  • (\beta_{\text{exp}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.5)): allows exposure to scale risk but shrinks towards “no effect” if signal is weak.

Implementation: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo in PyMC with 4 chains, sufficient warmup, and standard diagnostics (R-hat, effective sample size, energy plots, trace plots) to ensure we are sampling from a stable posterior.

Why this structure?#

Key points:

  1. Counts are conditional on exposure.
    A segment with 0 incidents and 0 traffic tells you very little about safety.
    A segment with 0 incidents and heavy traffic is actually informative.

  2. Partial pooling across roads and days.
    Each road and each day borrows strength from the others. You avoid wild swings in risk estimates after a handful of incidents or a short quiet period.

  3. Latent risk surface.
    The parameter (\mu_i) (or (\mu_i) per unit exposure) is our estimate of the underlying risk, even when observed counts are temporarily zero.

  4. Explicit assumptions.

    • Poisson counts given the latent rate.
    • Log-linear relationship with exposure.
    • Road and day effects are approximately Gaussian deviations.
    • Reporting and exposure are encoded (imperfectly) in exposure.

The point is not that this model is “true,” but that it is less naive than eyeballing raw counts or simple last-48-hours frequencies.

Results#

From this model, we obtain:

  • Posterior draws of (\mu_i) for every segment and day.

  • Segment-level summaries:

    [ \hat{\mu}_{\text{seg}} = \mathbb{E}[\mu_i \mid \text{road}, \text{segment}] ]

  • Maps that shade each segment by its posterior mean latent risk.

Even in a minimal simulated Extremiststan scenario, several useful patterns emerge:

  1. Roads with recent “silence” can still have elevated latent risk.
    If a road historically has high incidents and exposure suddenly drops (police withdrawal, media blackout, fewer travelers), the model does not treat “0 incidents yesterday” as strong evidence of safety.
    It sees:

    • lower exposure,
    • past high risk,
    • similarity to other dangerous segments, and it keeps the latent rate elevated.

  2. Noisy spikes get shrunk.
    If one segment has a short-lived spike but:

    • other segments on the same road are quiet,
    • or similar days show moderate risk, the hierarchical structure pulls that spike back toward the road-level average.
      This avoids overreacting to one-off events.

  3. Maps become decision tools, not just pictures.
    Plotting the posterior mean latent risk by segment creates a risk surface you can use:

    • identify consistently dangerous stretches,
    • compare alternative routes on a comparable scale,
    • explain to non-technical stakeholders that “these colors are not counts, they are best guesses of underlying danger after adjusting for exposure and noise.”

  4. Posterior predictive checks keep you honest.
    We simulate new incident patterns from the fitted model and compare them to observed totals by road.

    • If the observed totals fall comfortably inside the model’s predictive distribution, our structure is at least compatible with the data.
    • If not, the model is missing something (e.g., spatial dependence, temporal persistence, changes in tactics, reporting bias) and should be revised.

This is the core mental move: treat observed incidents as partial, biased evidence about a hidden process, not as the process itself.

Conclusion#

“Did anything bad happen on that road yesterday?” is the wrong first question.

Better questions:

  • “Given everything we know about traffic, reporting, and past activity, what is the latent risk of that road right now?”
  • “How does that risk compare to the alternatives, after adjusting for exposure and shrinkage?”
  • “How sensitive are our conclusions to modeling assumptions?”

The Extremiststan example shows how a small hierarchical Poisson model already cures several common failure modes:

  • Over-trusting short-term quiet periods.
  • Ignoring exposure and observability.
  • Overreacting to small-sample spikes.
  • Pretending raw counts are ground truth.

This is not just about war.

The same mindset applies when you:

  • pick fraud thresholds from historical flags,
  • allocate cybersecurity monitoring across services,
  • monitor adverse events in healthcare,
  • prioritize inspections for infrastructure.

If you act on raw counts, you are implicitly using a model anyway. It is just an undocumented, brittle, and usually wrong one.

Write the model down. Make the latent risk explicit. Check it. Update it.

The body bags in this story are synthetic. In many domains, the consequences are not.
